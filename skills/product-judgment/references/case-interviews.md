# Case Interviews: Learn Product Judgment from Senior PMs

You don't learn product judgment from books. You learn it from senior PMs who've made many calls and can tell you **why** they made them. This guide is a 30-minute structured conversation that extracts their judgment.

## The goal

A great case interview gets you 3-5 concrete product lessons the senior PM has internalized but rarely articulates. The format works because:

- Open questions ("tell me about your work") get you stories. Stories are the wrong unit.
- Specific questions ("walk me through a feature you killed and why") get you lessons. Lessons are the right unit.
- 30 minutes is enough. Senior PMs are busy. Anything longer becomes a mentorship.

## How to ask (the script)

Send something like this:

> I'm trying to get better at product judgment, not product execution. I'd love 30 min to ask you about 3-4 specific calls you made — what you decided, what you considered, and what you'd do differently. No prep needed; we'll just go from memory.

The phrase "judgment, not execution" is important. It signals you're not asking for advice on your project. You're asking to extract their pattern library.

## The 5 questions (pick 3-4, save 5 min for follow-ups)

1. **"Walk me through a feature you killed — one that had internal support, but you decided not to ship. What made you decide?"**
   - Extracts: their threshold for "real need" vs. "internal politics"

2. **"What's a product decision you got wrong, where the process was good but the outcome was bad?"**
   - Extracts: humility, knowing where their framework fails, intellectual honesty

3. **"When you're undecided between two options, what's the tiebreaker?"**
   - Extracts: their personal heuristic. This is the most valuable question.

4. **"A PM you respect disagrees with you about a feature. How do you decide who's right?"**
   - Extracts: their epistemology — how they handle disagreement

5. **"What's the most common mistake you see junior PMs make when trying to develop product sense?"**
   - Extracts: their mental model of the learning curve. Often self-revealing.

## The follow-up traps (use them)

When they give a vague answer ("we just did a lot of user research"), push:

- "What's the *one* finding from that research that changed your mind?"
- "What did you almost miss that you caught at the last minute?"
- "If you had to defend the opposite decision, what's the strongest case?"

The strongest case against their own view is the most useful thing you can extract. It tells you what they were *really* worried about.

## After the conversation

Within 24 hours, write a 1-page summary. Use this format:

```markdown
# [Senior PM name] — Product judgment notes

## Three things they kept saying / believing:
1. ...
2. ...
3. ...

## One thing I disagreed with:
...

## One question I should have asked but didn't:
...

## One thing I'll try in my own work this week:
...
```

The "try this week" line is what converts the conversation from networking to learning.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Don't ask them to review your product.** That turns it into consulting, not an interview.
- **Don't take notes on your laptop visibly.** It signals "this is work" not "I'm curious." Use a notepad or ask permission to record.
- **Don't ask "what's your framework?"** Senior PMs don't think in frameworks. Ask about specific decisions.
- **Don't try to ask all 5 questions.** Pick 3-4 and go deep. The depth is the point.
- **Don't fake expertise.** If you don't know what they mean, say so. Senior PMs respect honesty.

## Who to ask

Start with PMs 1-2 levels above you at your company, or PMs at companies you admire. Cold outreach works if you're specific. LinkedIn DMs that say "I read your post about X and want to learn from your product decisions" get responses. Generic asks ("could I pick your brain?") don't.
