# Decision Journal Template

A simple template for tracking product decisions and their outcomes. The point is not the journal itself — the point is that **decisions you write down get better, and decisions you revisit get sharper**.

## When to use

- Whenever you make a non-trivial product decision (especially a "gut call" that you can't fully justify)
- Whenever you predict an outcome ("this will increase retention by X%")
- Whenever you override a framework or a senior's advice

## How to use

Copy this template once per decision. Keep them in a single folder (`decisions/YYYY-MM-DD-name.md` is a good convention). Review your past decisions monthly — that's where the real learning happens.

## Template

```markdown
# [Short decision title]

**Date:** YYYY-MM-DD
**Context:** [What product, what stage, who's the user]

## The decision
[What I chose to do. One sentence.]

## What I predicted would happen
[Specific, falsifiable. "Retention will go up 5%" not "users will like it more."]

## Why I made this call
[2-4 reasons. Force ranking — which reason is most important?]

## What I considered and rejected
[Other options I considered. Why I rejected them. This is more important than the reasons I chose — it surfaces your blind spots.]

## What I'd need to learn to be more confident
[What's the unknown? If I had more time, what would I study?]

---

## Outcome (fill in 30 / 60 / 90 days later)

**What actually happened:** [...]
**Was I right?** yes / partially / no
**What I learned:** [...]
**What I'd do differently next time:** [...]
```

## Review ritual (monthly, 30 minutes)

1. Open the folder of decisions from the past 90 days.
2. For each one, ask: **what pattern is here?**
3. Look for: decisions that worked for the wrong reason, decisions that failed despite good process, decisions where the data was misleading.
4. Write a 5-line summary: "Three things I keep getting wrong: ..."

That summary is the asset. The decisions are the input.

## Anti-patterns

- **Don't write a decision journal to feel productive.** The journal is a tool for self-correction, not a substitute for making the call.
- **Don't skip the "considered and rejected" section.** It's the most important part. That's where your actual thinking lives.
- **Don't use vague predictions.** "Users will like it" is not a prediction. "Day-7 retention will hold at 35% or above" is.
