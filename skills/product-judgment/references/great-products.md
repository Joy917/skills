# Great Products to Study

A starter list of products worth studying for product judgment training. The list is **deliberately not exhaustive** — pick the ones you actually use, not the ones that "sound impressive."

## Why study existing products

Most product judgment comes from pattern recognition. You can't recognize a good (or bad) call if you haven't seen many. The fastest way to build a mental library is to **study what already shipped, with rigor**.

The exercise is the same for every product:
1. What's the user's actual job-to-be-done?
2. What's the one design decision that defines this product?
3. What did they **not** do? (Negative space is taste.)
4. If I had to bet, why does this work (or not)?

## Week 2 — "Bad" samples (pick 7)

These are products that failed, declined, or had famous missteps. The point is to extract the lesson, not to dunk.

| # | Product | What to study | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Google+ | Real-name policy. Forcing social graph. | Misread user privacy preferences |
| 2 | Quibi | $1.75B for short content on mobile. | Misread context of use (commute ≠ couch) |
| 3 | Microsoft Zune | Late entry, weird sync model. | Wrong wedge vs. iPod ecosystem |
| 4 | Parler | "Free speech" positioning. | Built a feature (free speech) that the market didn't actually want at scale |
| 5 | Amazon Fire Phone | Dynamic perspective without a use case. | Tech-first, problem-never |
| 6 | Google Glass | Form factor vs. social cost. | Solved a problem nobody had, ignored the social cost |
| 7 | MoviePass | Unsustainable unit economics. | Confused "users want it cheap" with "users want unlimited" |

(Again, substitute. The lesson is in the analysis, not the brand name.)

## How to do the exercise

For each product, **spend 30 minutes** answering:

1. What user problem did this product exist to solve?
2. What was the single most important design / strategic decision?
3. What did the product **deliberately not do**? (What's missing?)
4. If I had to bet on why it worked (or didn't), what would I say?
5. **One thing I learned that I can apply to my own product.**

Write each answer. Don't just think them. The act of writing forces precision.

## After week 2

You now have a structured library of 14 cases. Look for patterns:
- What do the "good" products have in common that I haven't articulated?
- What do the "bad" products have in common?
- Where does my own product fall on these axes?

That synthesis is your first real upgrade in judgment. The cases are inputs. The pattern is the output.
